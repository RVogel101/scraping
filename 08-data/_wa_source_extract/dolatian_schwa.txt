Introduction
● Title: Orthography to Phonology: Constraints on the Armenian schwa
● Orthography: Look at rule of spelling-pronunciation in Armenian
→ Schwa epenthesis
● Phonology: tendencies in pronunciation follow phonological principles
● These principles are:
/uni25B8Pure phonology: directional syllabiﬁcation
/uni25B8Morphology: alignment to morpheme boundaries
/uni25B8Morphophonology: cyclicity and allomorphy
3
Why orthography?
● Orthography often used to motivate phonological analyses
/uni25B8Cross-linguistically (Chomsky und Halle, 1968) and Armenian (Vaux, 1998)
● Open question if orthographic representations (ORs) match:
/uni25B8Underlying Representations, or
/uni25B8Surface Representations, or
/uni25B8Intermediate Representation (Sproat, 2000)
● Safety measure:
/uni25B8Instead of looking at UR->SR transformations, will look at OR->SR
/uni25B8Any proposed URs are up for debate, but not the ORs + SRs
/uni25B8In fact, will show that ORsprobably reﬂect URs

Armenian orthography
● Armenian is Indo-European isolate with it’s own script
● Orthography captures all phonemic contrasts
● Predictable to go from orthography to pronunciation
● Phonology: canonical 6-vowel system
[a] [u] [o] [e] [i] [@]
● Orthography: many-to-one mapping from grapheme to phoneme but still
predictable
<a> < ow> < , o> < , e> < i> < >
<a> < u> < o> < e> < i> < @>
● Transliteration removes many-to-one mapping because tangential for talk

Unconscious
● Q1: Is it part of mental grammars, or just a consciously learned process?
● It’s not consciously learned
● Textbooks and teaching grammars often provide ‘general rules’ for schwa
epenthesis based
● But the rules are simple
/uni25B8"If CCCCV, sometimes schwa after C1 and/or C3"
● Does this mean that speakers don’t learn a schwa-epenthesis process?

Grammatical
● Q2: If part of grammar, is it part of orthography or also phonology?
● It has to at least be part of orthography as a ‘grammar’
● Given a nonce word or low-frequency word, a speaker will pronounce this
word as expected based all the relevant phonological/morphological
constraints
● Vaux tested a speaker on a lit of 100 words
● The speaker only recognized 20% of the words
● For the remaining 80%, he correctly prononounced all but 7 of them
● For the 7 ‘errors’, he had given the ‘wrong’ morphological parse

URs?
● So there is a grammatical process of schwa epenthesis from orthography

● Q3: Does it say anything about URs?
● Inconclusive yes
● Armenian script was created for Classical Armenian in 3/4th century
(Sanjian, 1996)
● The grapheme was used for schwas, but not inside consonant clusters
● Both Classical and Modern Armenian had ORs with clusters that are
broken up in pronunciation
● Virtually the same epenthesis process in Classical and Modern (Pierce,
2007)
● What this means? The original creators of the script must have thought
that their own surface schwas were predictable to skip writing
